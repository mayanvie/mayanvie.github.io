Your brain doesn't start at zero — it starts with a schema

The brain doesn't like starting from zero — it starts with a "schema." An essay on language learning, especially the part about remembering vocabulary.

A few days ago, while despairing over how slowly I was learning Chinese, I went on YouTube to see how the rest of the world studies — and maybe steal a trick or two.

Someone said that on some days they skim over 1,000 words, and on other days they study pinyin, meanings, and example sentences all at once.

Wow.

I'm a simple person. So I copied them.

But 1,000 words is a lot. Each session I only skim about 500 Chinese characters. Then I pick around 30 words to study properly. Before I start, I still skim through them first.

Midway through studying, I had a tiny realization.

Although skimming didn't help me remember much, when I actually sat down to learn (rewriting and memorizing), I would sometimes vaguely remember a few of those words. It made me feel a little less discouraged while studying, and occasionally there was a small relief or spark of interest whenever I ran into a word I half-remembered.

I asked ChatGPT what was going on. It pointed me toward the Activation of prior knowledge principle.

So instead of going back to my desk, I dropped everything to write this blog.

What is prior knowledge? Why activate it?

In 1932, psychologist Frederic Bartlett ran a classic experiment to understand how people remember. He asked participants to read the story "The War of the Ghosts" from North American Indigenous peoples.

Of course, this was a deliberate choice — the story contains many elements unfamiliar to the participants.

After some time, he asked them to retell it, and guess what — they unconsciously changed the content to fit their own memories and familiar experiences, especially making the story more logical than the original. For example, they cut many mystical details and strange names, changed "canoe" to "boat", turned "seal hunting" into "fishing", and so on.

Okay, what does this mean?

Hmm.

Our brains don't take in knowledge like copy-paste. They always interpret new information based on what we already know.

Everything we've accumulated — understanding and experience — is prior knowledge.

Prior knowledge isn't scattered or chaotic. It's connected much like tiles in a domino game — related pieces stick together, forming meaningful clusters called schemas.

Unlike dominoes, which can only run in a straight line, our brains can process multiple schema clusters (schemata) in parallel at once — and shift or adjust them when needed.

One small clarification so we don't mix things up: a schema isn't just knowledge. It's also the way knowledge is organized and arranged so it makes sense and can be understood.

A light example to help you picture a schema:

When you hear the phrase "school," a schema might pop up with a blackboard, teachers, desks, notebooks, adorable students… or a schema of memories with friends, the feeling of walking to school every day… If someone told you that school is where Skibidi Toilets gather, your brain would need extra time to rebuild the diagram.

Sometimes, new knowledge is completely alien to our existing schemas, and the brain may refuse to take it in.

As in Frederic Bartlett's experiment, the image of "seal hunting" wasn't part of the participants' culture. So some people forgot it entirely; others' brains "edited" it into something more familiar or logical (like "fishing").

The concept of "schema" was later defined by David Rumelhart as follows:

"A schema is a data structure for representing the generic concepts stored in memory." (Rumelhart, 1980, p. 34)

Schemas come in many types, but I won't go deep here (honestly because I'm too lazy to research further).

You can imagine a schema like a flexible network where knowledge, experience, emotion, and context are linked together to create coherence.

Information attached to a schema will:

  • be encoded more deeply
  • move into long-term memory
  • be easier to retrieve when needed

Thanks to this we can learn faster, remember longer, understand deeper, and even reason further instead of rote memorization.

In short, activating prior knowledge can be seen as warming up schemas for learning.

How do you apply "schema" to language learning?

The hard parts

  • Remember that schemas are built from prior knowledge — and prior knowledge isn't always correct or complete. Wrong prior knowledge creates wrong schemas that still feel (very) consistent and logical. Their job is to simplify the world — and that simplicity comes at the cost of a narrower view. When you mention "Earth," a child's schema might be a blue ball; an adult's might be a planet in the solar system, gravity, atmosphere…
  • Also, schemas aren't always activated at the right moment. Maybe we haven't encoded that prior knowledge strongly enough, or sometimes the context has changed.

The other day I ran into the word 目标 (goal) so many times in one article that I almost had it down. But a few days later, in a different piece, I saw 标准 (standard) — same 标 character — and I kept feeling like I knew it but couldn't recall it.

So in summary: learning through schemas is quite useful, but relying on them too much can also make you wronger and wronger — or skip unfamiliar knowledge and other good methods.

How to use it

(The rest is my own thinking, based on the characteristics of schemas above.)

  • Sometimes our prior knowledge is already enough. I may never have seen a vacuum cleaner, but just hearing the name I can picture what it does and vaguely guess its shape.
  • But if a schema isn't there yet, we can adjust or build one — for example when learning the name of an unfamiliar city, look up scenery or a few interesting facts online so the brain can gradually form sensible links.
  • And sometimes new knowledge goes against our schemas. Instead of rejecting it or skimming past, we can try doubting and asking: "Is my schema actually right?" — which ties into belief, and most of our built-in beliefs are hard to change.

A few small tips

  • When you meet new knowledge, ask yourself what similar things you've known before.
  • Learn vocabulary by related themes (mind-map style) or as word families around one root.
  • Find points to compare, contrast, and analyze similarities or opposites between new and old vocabulary.
  • With Chinese specifically, there's the fun option of explaining the imagery inside a character.
  • Learn whole sentences or passages instead of isolated words — you get story context and naturally repeat basic vocabulary.
  • Write mini stories that tie vocabulary to personal context. The sillier and weirder, the better.
  • When reading in a foreign language, reading headings carefully first helps the brain build a basic context.
  • Learning and using something right away can activate schemas more effectively.

Ideal conditions for activating prior knowledge

(This section is a bit more personal — I wrote it for myself.)

In the end, I don't think any method is truly "optimal." It might be optimal for some mechanism in the brain, or for your time or energy — but it can't be optimal for a whole complicated human who is sunny one moment and rainy the next, lazy then diligent, happy then sad — plus all the outside conditions, like today in Saigon it's so hot nobody can study properly.

Learner mindset

  • You're focused fairly steadily on what you're studying — able to "detach" from distractions and drift into the imagination of related schemas.
  • Your mind is open, relaxed, and still has room for something new.
  • You're interested — for me that's browsing notes and highlights on Pinterest or Xiaohongshu; for you it might be something else.

Learning context

  • Right after watching a Chinese video or song with interesting or unfamiliar words.
  • Before or after a conversation with a native speaker.
  • Or after a game, when a vague confusing word makes you want to look it up immediately.

I think those are good moments to lean on schema mechanisms; when my head is overloaded or tired, I just want to sit and write characters casually — remember what I can.

And finally, the story I told at the opening about skimming before studying isn't a textbook example of schema activation. Sorry for leading with it — but a teacher once told me that when you've spent effort researching a hypothesis and discover it's actually meaningless, that's still a meaningful finding.
