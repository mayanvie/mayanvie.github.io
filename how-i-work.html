How I work

Structure matters — but so does how safe someone feels on day one. I design for both: clear modules and checklists, plus room to practice before it counts. That applies whether I'm onboarding guides or keeping a program running month to month.

Understand learners

Who they are, what they already know — and what makes them avoid asking questions in week one.

Break it into steps

Big picture first, then detail. Days, modules, checklists — so people aren't drowning before they start.

Design docs & activities

Clear language, real practice (not slides alone), and a simple way to check if someone can actually do it.

Track & follow up

Checklists, trackers, reminders — the unglamorous part that stops things from quietly falling apart.

Adjust from feedback

What did people still ask twice? That's usually where the next edit needs to go.

Tools I use

Google Sheets Notion Canva Google Docs Confluence-style docs Comfortable learning new systems quickly

I pick tools that keep content findable and programs repeatable — not because a new app is exciting, but because the team needs to find things again next month.

LMS & corporate systems

Honestly — I haven't run an enterprise LMS in production yet. But I already think in terms of what ops teams need: structured content, enrollments, completion tracking, and an experience that doesn't confuse the person who just wants to know what to do today.

I'm ready to learn tools like iLearn (or similar) quickly. The concepts aren't new to me: modules, activities, completion criteria. The software is.

On my Substack I write a lot about input vs. output in learning — listening alone doesn't get you speaking. I try to build programs the same way: people need to do the thing early, even when it's messy.
