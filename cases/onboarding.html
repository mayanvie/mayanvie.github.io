case study

Art Experience Guide — onboarding program

Onboarding design 7 days · 26 hours Reconstructed portfolio piece

Role: Learning & onboarding design (from my time as an Art Experience Guide)
Organization: A creative exhibition (name kept confidential)

Portfolio note: This program is reconstructed from gaps I saw on the job — not from a post-launch report. I don't have rollout metrics yet. The design logic is real; the numbers would come after a pilot.
Learners

New Art Experience Guides, usually 18–30 — students or career changers from arts, social sciences, or languages. They knew basic company info but not workflows, org structure, exhibition content, or how to handle surprises.

Problem

New hires learned from whoever was available that day — so quality shifted every hiring round. The thing that broke people in week one? Detailed zone checklists. Too much to hold in your head while everything else is new.

What I did

Designed a 7-day program (5 modules + practice + assessment), including learner analysis, learning objectives, program outline, and one fully written module (Customer Service) with activities and pass criteria.

Design goal

After the first week: set up zones on time, guide full tours alone, answer basic exhibition questions, contact the right teams, and handle common guest situations in English or with support tools.

Why I designed it this way

Before this, onboarding mostly meant shadowing whoever was on shift. That works until it doesn't — and when the exhibition hires often and updates content weekly, "who trained you" starts to matter more than it should.

Simply put: new guides needed a stable path from big picture to detail. Not an information dump on day one.

I ordered modules Orientation → Product Knowledge → Daily Operations → Customer Service → Problem Solving, then built in shadowing and a supervised solo tour before the final assessment. Learning and doing had to happen in the same week — not "study first, speak later."

Learner analysis (summary)

  • Environment: Content and procedures change often. Guides have to self-update — there's no single static handbook that stays true for long.
  • Motivation: Extra income, peers, better communication — including with international guests.
  • Gap: Checklist-heavy zones plus training that depended on which colleague was free. Easy to feel like you're on the outside looking in.

Learning objectives

After the program, a new guide should be able to:

  1. Set up their zone from the checklist before opening — without asking a senior.
  2. Guide the full visitor flow per zone without backup.
  3. Explain basic exhibition content (artist, phases, highlights, projection techniques) without constantly checking notes.
  4. Reach the right internal contact when something comes up.
  5. Handle routine communication with international guests (English or support tools).
  6. Update themselves when content or process changes — without repeated reminders.

Program outline

Module Focus Hours When
1 — Orientation Company, culture, process, teams 3 Day 1 AM
2 — Product knowledge Exhibition, artists, works, ticketing 4 Day 1 PM – Day 2 AM
3 — Daily operations Zone setup, checklists, equipment care 3 Day 2 PM
4 — Customer service Guiding guests, situations, communication 6 Days 3–4
5 — Problem solving Updates, incidents, cross-team contact 2 Day 5 AM
Practice Shadowing + supervised solo tour 6 Days 5 PM – 6
Assessment Quiz + role play + final review 2 Day 7

Total: 7 days · 26 hours

Module highlight — Customer Service (fully developed)

This is the module I wrote out completely — because guides are the main guest touchpoint, and real situations don't show up in a slide deck. You need practice, and a low-stakes way to mess up before the floor gets busy.

Module objectives

  • Guide visitors through each zone without senior support.
  • Classify incidents and escalate to the right person within 10–15 minutes.

Content areas

  • Welcome: Quick read of the group, natural greeting, clarify needs (ticket status, prior knowledge).
  • Guiding: Paths for guests with/without tickets; professional tone; radio handoffs between zones.
  • Questions: When to answer from reference files vs. escalate; never guess; don't keep guests waiting over 5 minutes.
  • International guests: Prepared English phrases + translation/radio backup.
  • Incidents: Guest issues → team channel; policy → manager; technical → tech team.

Activities & assessment

  • Multiple-choice quiz after training.
  • Role play with manager as different guest types (curious, hesitant, ready to tour).
  • Pass criteria: ≥80% on quiz; ≥70% on role play (attitude, accuracy, response time); supervised solo tour without basic errors (missed zones, wrong info, no response >5 min).

In a corporate L&D / ops setting, I would add

If this lived inside a bigger org, here's what I'd want around it — the part that turns a good doc into something you can actually run at scale:

  • Registration and completion tracking in an LMS (e.g. iLearn or similar)
  • Automated reminders and manager dashboards when someone's stuck on a step
  • Standard templates in Confluence / SharePoint for each cohort
  • KPI reporting: time-to-independent tour, quiz pass rates, how often new hires still ping seniors for the same questions
  • A clear owner for quarterly content updates when the exhibition or policy shifts

Reflection

I built this from my own onboarding friction — and from watching cohort after cohort train differently depending on who was around. The exhibition kept hiring and kept changing content; "just learn from whoever's on shift" wasn't going to hold.

What I'd do next with stakeholder access: pilot one cohort, watch what people still ask twice, track how long until a solo tour feels okay — then tighten the modules based on that. Progress in learning is often hard to see day to day. You usually notice it when you look back.
